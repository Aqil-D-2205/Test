Cosine Function

Transformation

I chose this photo taken in Subang Jaya, Malaysia, because it captures a fun, casual moment with my friend, and the smooth, wavy curve formed by our bodies lying back‑to‑back perfectly matches the shape of a cosine function. I started with the parent function 𝑦 = cos(𝑥), which has a regular, symmetrical wave shape. Comparing it to the curve in the image, I observed the wave was vertically stretched, shifted horizontally to the right, and translated upward — no reflection was needed, as the curve rose and fell in the same direction as the original cosine graph.

At first, it was difficult to determine the vertical stretch factor because the curve was not positioned near the origin, making its amplitude hard to measure accurately. To simplify, I mentally shifted the curve so its midline aligned with the 𝑥‑axis, allowing me to focus only on its height and shape without the complexity of translations. By comparing the maximum and minimum heights, I determined a vertical stretch by a factor of 2 fit best, and refined this value until the curve closely overlapped the outline of our bodies in the photo.

Once the amplitude was set, I moved the curve back into its correct position: shifted 1 unit to the right to align the peak of the wave with the highest point of the curve, then shifted 3 units upward to centre the wave properly. This resulted in my final function: 𝑦 = 2 cos(𝑥 - 1) + 3


Summary of Transformations:
- Vertical stretch by a factor of 2 → 2 cos(𝑥)
- Horizontal translation 1 unit to the right → 2 cos(𝑥 - 1)
- Vertical translation 3 units upward → 2 cos(𝑥 - 1) + 3

Domain and Range

The domain of my function is D:{𝑥 ∈ ℝ|−6.835 ≤ 𝑥 ≤ 8.33}, restricted to the visible portion of the curve formed by our bodies in the photograph. The key reference points from the graph are (-9.1,1.32), (-2.14,3), (1, 5), and (10.42,1), which define the start, midline, peak, and end of the visible curve. Ideally, I would have captured the full periodic wave to model its complete shape, but this domain covers only the section shown — so the model applies only to this visible part.

The parent function y = cos(𝑥) has a domain D:{𝑥 ∈ ℝ} and range R:{𝑦 ∈ ℝ|−1 ≤ y ≤ 1}; it is defined for all real 𝑥-values and oscillates continuously between -1 and 1, with no asymptotes or discontinuities. My transformed function also has no undefined points, and within this restricted domain, the curve remains fully continuous — so I do not need to consider discontinuities when describing the domain and range for this situation.

Since the cosine function is smooth and continuous over this entire interval, the range can be determined by evaluating the function at the domain’s endpoints and its maximum/minimum values. The lowest values are approximately 1.00–1.32, and the highest value at the peak (1,5) is 5.00, giving an overall range of approximately R:{𝑦 ∈ ℝ|1.00 ≤ 𝑦 ≤ 5.00}.

Tangent Function

Transformation

I chose this fun photo taken in Subang Jaya, Malaysia, because the smooth, sweeping curve my friend and I made while lying side‑by‑side looks exactly like the shape of a tangent graph — it’s such a cool way to turn our relaxed pose into a math model! I started by looking at the parent tangent function, 𝑦 = tan(𝑥). It has a very distinct shape: it rises steeply from left to right, repeats every 180 degree, and has vertical asymptotes — invisible lines where the graph shoots upward or downward and is not defined.

Compared to that parent graph, the curve we formed was much gentler, not nearly as steep, taller overall, shifted to the right, and sat lower down. So I adjusted the parent function step‑by‑step to match perfectly. First, I stretched it vertically by a factor of 5 — this made it taller and less steep, matching the gentle slope of our bodies. Next, I shifted the whole graph 2 units to the right so its centre lined up exactly with the highest point of our curve. Finally, I moved it 2 units downward so it sat at the correct height to trace our pose. The final function I ended up with is: 𝑦 = 5 tan(𝑥 - 2) - 2


Summary of Transformations:
- Vertical stretch by a factor of 5: 𝑦 = 5 tan(𝑥)
- Horizontal translation 2 units right: 𝑦 = 5 tan(𝑥 - 2)
- Vertical translation 2 units down: 𝑦 = 5 tan(𝑥 - 2) - 2

Domain and Range

I titled my graph Body Length as a Function of Horizontal Distance Between the Two Students. The 𝑦‑axis represents the horizontal distance between us, measured in units, while the 𝑦‑axis shows the corresponding vertical height or length of our bodies forming the curve.

Looking again at the parent tangent function 𝑦 = tan(𝑥): its domain includes all real numbers except 𝑥 = π/2+nπ, where those vertical asymptotes occur — at these points, the function has no defined value and is discontinuous. Its range covers all real numbers from negative infinity to positive infinity, meaning it can go infinitely low or infinitely high.

Titled Body Length as a Function of Horizontal Distance Between the Two Students, this graph uses 𝑦 = 5 tan(𝑥 - 2) - 2 to model our pose.
